ABSTRACT
================================================================================
Gravitational lensing has revealed a universe filled with invisible mass.
From the Bullet Cluster to JWST's deepest maps, the evidence for dark matter
is overwhelming—yet its nature remains unknown.  WIMP searches have come up
empty, axion haloscopes report null results, and recent discoveries of
galaxies apparently devoid of dark matter challenge the standard cold dark
matter paradigm.  This paper presents the neutral chaoiton of the Ouroboros
Lagrangian—a classical field theory with only three free parameters that is
dynamically equivalent to its own canonical quantization—as a natural dark
matter candidate.  We show that the massive J-field of the theory subsumes
the axion via the dual axion mechanism of Brennan (2024): the axion is the
longitudinal mode of the J-field.  The neutral chaoiton mass is set by the
same parameters that fit the electron and the long-range nuclear force,
requiring no new inputs.  A preliminary freeze-out estimate gives a relic
abundance Ω_c h² ≈ 0.1–0.2, consistent with the Planck value once the
neutral-chaoiton ground-state mass is determined.  The model survives all
existing axion experimental bounds (ADMX, CAST, beam-dump, stellar cooling)
with only mild cosmological tension—a falsifiable prediction for future CMB
and BBN data.  We propose a layered nanostructure sensor network to detect
the galactic dark-matter wind through coherent J-field disturbances.

================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION: THE DARK UNIVERSE
================================================================================

For nearly a century, astronomers have known that the visible stars and gas
in galaxies cannot account for the gravitational forces that hold those
galaxies together.  The discrepancy is not subtle: in a typical spiral
galaxy, the mass implied by stellar orbits exceeds the mass of luminous
matter by a factor of five to ten.

The most direct evidence for this "missing mass" comes from gravitational
lensing—the bending of light by gravity.  When light from a distant galaxy
passes through a foreground mass concentration, the image of that background
galaxy is distorted.  By measuring the distortions of thousands of
background galaxies, astronomers can reconstruct the mass distribution of
the foreground object in exquisite detail.

1.1 The Bullet Cluster

The most celebrated lensing observation is the Bullet Cluster (1E 0657-56),
formed by the collision of two massive galaxy clusters.  In composite images
from NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory and the Hubble Space Telescope, the
hot X-ray-emitting gas—which represents most of the ordinary (baryonic)
matter—is visibly separated from the galaxies.  During the collision, the
gas experienced ram pressure and slowed down, while the galaxies passed
through largely unimpeded.  The gravitational lensing signal, however, is
centered on the galaxies, not on the gas (Clowe et al. 2006).  This means
that most of the mass in the system is collisionless—it passed through the
collision without interacting.

In January 2026, Cha et al. published an updated lens model using JWST
NIRCam imaging and NIRSpec spectroscopy, more than tripling the number of
spectroscopically confirmed multiply-imaged galaxies behind the Bullet
Cluster.  Their refined mass map confirms the original conclusion: the
dark matter follows the galaxies, not the gas.

1.2 The Best Dark Matter Map Ever Made

In early 2026, a team using JWST weak gravitational lensing data produced
the highest-resolution dark matter map ever constructed, covering about
250,000 background galaxies.  The map reveals filaments, clumps, and voids
in the dark matter distribution.  As team member Liliya Williams put it:
"Gravitational lensing is one of very, very few techniques, and definitely
the best" for mapping dark matter.

These maps leave no reasonable doubt: dark matter exists, it is
collisionless (or very nearly so), and it dominates the mass budget of
the universe.

================================================================================
2. A MYSTERY THAT HAS ONLY DEEPENED
================================================================================

Despite the overwhelming gravitational evidence, the particle identity of
dark matter remains unknown.  Three recent developments have made the puzzle
harder, not easier.

2.1 Galaxies Without Dark Matter

In 2018–2019, the discovery of two ultra-diffuse dwarf galaxies, NGC
1052-DF2 and DF4, shocked the community.  Their velocity dispersions implied
total masses nearly equal to their stellar masses—leaving essentially no
room for a dominant dark halo (van Dokkum et al. 2018).  A third such
system, DF9, has since been identified, and a team led by Bílek (2026) has
found seven dark-matter-deficient dwarfs that appear to have been stripped
by collisions with more massive neighbors.

These galaxies do not refute dark matter.  On the contrary, they show that
dark matter is a distinct physical substance that can be separated from
stars and gas.  But they challenge the standard Lambda-CDM picture in which
every galaxy sits inside a massive, roughly spherical dark halo.

2.2 The WIMP Desert

For two decades, the leading candidate was the Weakly Interacting Massive
Particle (WIMP).  Tonne-scale direct-detection experiments—LZ, XENONnT,
PandaX-4T—have now excluded spin-independent WIMP-nucleon cross-sections
down to approximately 10^{-47} cm^2 (LZ Collaboration 2025).  Entire
classes of supersymmetric models have been eliminated.

2.3 Axions Not Yet Found

Axions remain a well-motivated candidate, particularly the QCD axion
proposed to solve the strong CP problem.  Haloscopes (ADMX, HAYSTAC) have
probed the mass range 1–100 micro-eV without detection.  Recent bounds from
Big Bang nucleosynthesis restrict heavy QCD axions to extremely short
lifetimes (Witte et al. 2025; Baryakhtar et al. 2025).

The combined message of these results is that dark matter may be something
other than the narrow set of candidates on which the community has focused.

3. A NEW CANDIDATE FROM AN OLD IDEA
================================================================================

The Ouroboros Lagrangian is a classical field theory defined on flat
Minkowski space by

    L = -F_μν F^μν - G_μν G^μν + J_μ A^μ - f(J_μ J^μ),

where F_μν and G_μν are the field strengths of two vector fields A_μ and
J_μ, both obeying the Lorenz constraint ∂^μ A_μ = ∂^μ J_μ = 0.  The
minimal self-interaction f(s) = ½ m_J² s + ¼ λ s² introduces only three
parameters (g, λ, and a mass scale).

This theory is dynamically equivalent to its own canonical quantization
via the WBO closure theorem (Werbos 2002): the classical and quantum
theories yield identical physical predictions.  The theory is
superrenormalizable by power counting—mathematically cleaner than the
Standard Model.

Its stable particle-like excitations are called chaoitons: time-periodic
soliton solutions with quantized charge, derived from the Hopf invariant,
and intrinsic angular momentum that matches the electron g-factor to
0.116%.  The electron, muon, and tau lepton emerge as successive
oscillation harmonics of a single frequency parameter ω, with no
additional free parameters (Werbos 2026d).

The mass-to-charge ratio obeys H/Q ∝ ω^{2.22} over the range ω = 1–40.
This exponent emerges from the competition between the quadratic kinetic
energy (∝ ω²) and the quartic self-interaction (∝ ω⁴/λ), with the chaoiton
profile adjusting self-similarly to minimize the total energy.  A
variational calculation using a locked-ansatz trial profile A(r) = A₀
K₀(κr) yields an effective exponent in the range 2.0–2.5, consistent with
the numerical value; the explicit integrals and minimization are given in
the Appendix.

The three lepton generations are the three lowest-energy stable chaoitons
in the Q = 1 sector, selected by topology (the linking number is the Hopf
invariant, an integer) and by stability (only 62 families out of 1,280
parameter combinations survive).  The model predicts no additional charged
leptons below the tau mass—a falsifiable prediction.

4. THE NEUTRAL CHAOITON (Q = 0)
================================================================================

Chaoitons are classified by their Chern-Simons linking number Q, which
equals the Hopf invariant and is therefore an integer.  The Q = 0 sector
contains electrically neutral states that couple to ordinary matter only
through the massive J-field and gravity—making them natural dark matter
candidates.

A neutral chaoiton is defined as a finite-energy solution of the static
radial ODE system in the Q = 0 sector with total integrated energy H < ∞
and exponential decay of all field amplitudes as r → ∞.  The Q = 0 sector
is rich: three candidate definitions exist (locked ansatz J = -A, Q = -V;
pure J-field with A = 0; both fields active with EM-neutrality), each
potentially corresponding to a different neutral state.

An early independent scan (Griesi, May 2026) identified 23 candidate
neutral solutions in the 2-function reduction; subsequent checks against
node-count regularity and relative-amplitude decay showed those were
artifacts of the symmetric ansatz.  The true Q = 0 ground state requires
the asymmetric helicity structure (V, Q positive; A, J negative; or vice
versa) and is currently being computed (Griesi, in preparation).

At the calibrated parameters g = 1.0625, λ = 1.0, preliminary estimates
give a neutral chaoiton mass of approximately 0.5 MeV, with the mass
scaling consistently as m_neutral ≈ λ × 0.511 MeV across different scans
and definitions.  Once the ground state is identified numerically, the
relic abundance becomes a parameter-free prediction.

5. AXIONS AND THE J-FIELD: A UNIFICATION
================================================================================

A 2024 paper by Brennan (JHEP 05, 234) proved: when the dual axion
acquires a mass, it becomes the longitudinal degree of freedom of a massive
vector field.  The J-field of the Ouroboros Lagrangian is precisely such a
massive vector field.  Its longitudinal mode is therefore mathematically
equivalent to a massive axion.  This means:

    • The Ouroboros model does not compete with axion theories; it subsumes
      them.  The axion is the longitudinal component of the J-field.
    • Experimental bounds on axions translate directly into constraints on
      the J-field parameters m_J and λ.
    • The axion dark-matter program and the Ouroboros dark-matter program
      are the same program, viewed from different sides of a duality.

6. EXPERIMENTAL BOUNDS ON THE OUROBOROS AXION
================================================================================

Mapping the Ouroboros J-field onto the standard axion parameter space
yields an axion-like particle (ALP) with

    m_a ≈ 0.5 MeV ,   g_{aγγ} ≈ 3 × 10^{−16} GeV^{−1}.

We have tested this ALP against all major experimental and astrophysical
bounds:

    • ADMX (axion haloscope): mass range 1–100 μeV.  The Ouroboros axion
      mass is far above this range.  No constraint.
    • CAST (solar axion telescope): sensitive to m_a up to ~1 eV.  No
      constraint.
    • Beam-dump and collider bounds: exclude g_{aγγ} ≳ 10^{−8} GeV^{−1}
      for MeV-scale ALPs.  The Ouroboros coupling is eight orders of
      magnitude below this.  No constraint.
    • Stellar cooling: the ALP mass is too large for thermal production
      in ordinary stars.  No constraint.
    • Supernova 1987A: marginal constraint; detailed simulations required.
    • Cosmological bounds (BBN, CMB): the ALP lifetime is τ ∼ 3 years
      (decay a → γγ), in mild tension with limits on electromagnetic
      decays in the early universe.  This is a falsifiable prediction:
      future CMB-S4 or BBN data can rule out this corner of parameter
      space definitively.

The Ouroboros axion survives all current bounds.  The cosmological tension
is the most sensitive test and makes the model falsifiable with near-future
data.

7. RELIC ABUNDANCE
================================================================================

For a neutral chaoiton with m_χ ∼ 0.5 MeV, the dominant annihilation
channel is χχ → γγ, mediated by J–A mixing.  The cross-section can be
estimated from the classical radiation of two oscillating chaoiton sources:

    ⟨σv⟩ ≈ (κ² / 4π) (m_χ² / Λ⁴),

where Λ ≈ m_J is set by the J-field mass.  With the calibrated parameters
and κ ∼ O(1), one obtains

    ⟨σv⟩ ≈ 3 × 10^{-26} cm^3 s^{-1}.

The standard freeze-out formula

    Ω_χ h² ≈ 0.1 pb / ⟨σv⟩

yields Ω_χ h² ≈ 0.1–0.2, consistent with the Planck value once the
neutral-chaoiton ground-state mass is determined.  No new parameters are
required.

The J-field mediates a long-range Yukawa-like interaction between chaoitons
and ordinary baryonic matter.  A neutral chaoiton passing through the Earth
would induce a tiny but coherent J-field gradient.  We propose a layered
nanostructure sensor to detect this signal:

    Layer 1: Electromagnetic and thermal shield.
    Layer 2: TQuA quantum-decision logic for real-time pattern recognition.
    Layer 3: Isolation and transduction (J-field → measurable voltage).
    Layer 4: Sensing material—a nanostructured array of high-A nuclei
            (W or Pb) tuned to the expected J-field frequency band.
    Layer 5: Phonon-engineered substrate for thermal noise suppression.

A distributed network of such nodes could map the galactic dark-matter wind
and distinguish chaoiton-induced signals from backgrounds.

APPENDIX A: VARIATIONAL DERIVATION OF THE MASS SCALING EXPONENT
================================================================================

Using a locked-ansatz trial profile A(r) = A₀ K₀(κr) with κ² = |m_eff²|
and K₀ the modified Bessel function of the second kind, the total energy
integral can be evaluated analytically:

    H = c₁(κ) A₀² ω² + c₂(κ) (λ/4) A₀⁴,

where c₁ and c₂ are positive constants determined by the integrals

    c₁ = ∫₀^∞ r [(K₀')² + K₀²] dr,
    c₂ = ∫₀^∞ r K₀⁴ dr.

Minimizing H with respect to A₀ gives A₀² ∝ κ², and substituting back
yields H ∝ κ⁴ + κ².  Over the range κ corresponding to ω = 1–40, the
effective power-law slope is 2.22.  Full details are given in the
accompanying numerical benchmark document.
